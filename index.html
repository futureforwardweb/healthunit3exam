Holistic Health

```
Key Idea:

Holistic health considers all dimensions of a person's wellbeing — not just physical health — and recognises how social, mental, emotional, and spiritual factors interact.

Physical Health

The condition of the body — absence of disease, fitness, nutrition, sleep. Foundation for all other dimensions.

Mental / Emotional Health

Ability to manage emotions, cope with stress, maintain a positive outlook, and form a stable identity.

Social Health

Quality of relationships, ability to interact with others, sense of belonging, communication skills.

Spiritual Health

Sense of purpose, values, meaning — may or may not be religious. Includes cultural identity and connection.

Quantitative vs Qualitative Data

FeatureQuantitativeQualitative
TypeNumerical / measurableDescriptive / non-numerical
ExamplesMortality rates, BMI, survey scales, hospital admissionsInterviews, focus groups, personal accounts
StrengthsObjective, comparable, statistically analysableRich detail, captures lived experience
Use in HealthEpidemiological data, needs assessmentUnderstanding perceptions, barriers to care

Health Inequities

Health Inequity = unfair, avoidable differences in health outcomes between groups due to social, economic, or environmental disadvantages.

Health Inequality = measurable differences in health status (not necessarily unfair).

Factors Creating Inequity

  • Income and socioeconomic status
  • Geographic location (rural/remote)
  • Cultural and language barriers
  • Discrimination and stigma
  • Education and health literacy
  • Historical trauma (esp. First Nations)

Social Determinants of Health

  • Education
  • Employment & income
  • Housing and environment
  • Access to healthcare
  • Social inclusion
  • Early childhood development
```

Specific Populations — Access & Equity

```

Access

The ability of individuals and communities to obtain and use healthcare services when needed.

Equity

Just distribution of health resources, opportunities, and outcomes — everyone has the ability to achieve their highest level of health.

🏜️ Rural & Remote Australians ▼

Access Issues: Limited healthcare facilities, long travel distances, healthcare provider shortages, fewer specialists.

Equity Issues: Higher rates of chronic disease, lower insurance coverage, fewer specialised services, digital divide in telehealth.

👴 Elderly ▼

Access Issues: Mobility challenges, carer shortages, digital divide in telehealth services.

Equity Issues: Age-related discrimination (ageism), higher prevalence of chronic illnesses, financial constraints, isolation.

🪃 First Nations People ▼

Access Issues: Remote locations with limited health care infrastructure, underfunded services, cultural safety concerns.

Equity Issues: Historical trauma (intergenerational), higher prevalence of chronic diseases, lower life expectancy, systemic racism.

🏳️‍🌈 LGBTIQ+ ▼

Access Issues: Discrimination from healthcare providers, lack of culturally safe care, reluctance to disclose identity.

Equity Issues: Higher rates of mental health issues, homophobia/transphobia, conversion therapy harm, lack of inclusive health programs.

🔒 People in Prison ▼

Access Issues: Limited access to specialists, restricted healthcare choices, inadequate mental health support.

Equity Issues: Higher rates of mental illness, substance use disorders, and communicable diseases; social marginalisation post-release.

🌍 Culturally & Linguistically Diverse (CALD) ▼

Access Issues: Language barriers, lack of culturally appropriate services, low health literacy in English.

Equity Issues: Cultural differences in health beliefs, discrimination, socioeconomic disadvantage, reliance on community for health info.

👶 Children, Youth & Mothers/Babies ▼

Access Issues: Dependence on adults to access care, school-based health gap, maternal health service gaps in remote areas.

Equity Issues: Developmental impacts from unmet needs, childhood poverty, adverse childhood experiences (ACEs).

Epidemiological Data — used to identify health needs of specific populations: includes morbidity (illness rates), mortality (death rates), and related characteristics of populations.
```

Types of Needs

```
Purpose:

Used in healthcare, social services, and community development to assess and address people's requirements — identifying gaps, prioritising resources, and planning services effectively.

Felt Need

Felt Need

Definition: A subjective need an individual perceives or feels they have — not yet acted on.

Example: Person with chronic back pain feels they need physiotherapy but hasn't sought treatment.

How to determine: Community surveys, focus groups, community meetings, social media monitoring.

Expressed Need

Expressed Need

Definition: A felt need that has been acted upon — the individual has taken steps to seek help.

Example: Person with back pain visits physiotherapist, expressing need for treatment.

How to determine: Service usage data, waiting lists, hospital admissions, demand for public programs.

Normative Need

Normative Need

Definition: A need defined by experts or standards — based on public health guidelines or best practices.

Example: Recommended daily fruit & vegetable intake from Australian Dietary Guidelines.

How to determine: Official health guidelines, expert consultation, epidemiological data, legal requirements.

Comparative Need

Comparative Need

Definition: Determined by comparing groups to identify gaps in service provision.

Example: Rural town with no mental health services compared to an urban area that has them.

How to determine: Compare healthcare access, government reports, health statistics, best practices from other communities.

```

Needs Assessment

```
Purpose:

Systematic process used to identify and address health-related issues within a population. Assists decision-makers to allocate resources efficiently and design interventions to improve health outcomes and reduce inequities.

Identifying Health Issues

Recognising health concerns affecting a specific population using data. e.g. high rates of diabetes in a community.

Analysis of the Problem

Examining causes and risk factors; assessing barriers to healthcare access. e.g. lack of healthy food options and exercise facilities.

Prioritising Issues

Ranking health problems based on urgency, impact, and feasibility using community input, stakeholder engagement and data. e.g. diabetes prioritised as highly prevalent and preventable.

Setting Goals (SMART)

Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, Time-bound goals aligned with public health guidelines. e.g. Reduce diabetes by 10% over 5 years.

Determining Strategies

Evidence-based interventions — policy changes, community programs, education campaigns. e.g. introduction of subsidised healthy food programs.

Developing Action Plans

Creating timelines, delegating responsibilities, allocating resources — engaging health professionals, community leaders, government agencies.

Evaluating Outcomes

Measuring success through epidemiological data and feedback. Adjusting strategies. e.g. tracking diabetes screening rates.

```

Actions & Strategies to Address Health Inequity

```

Improving Access to Healthcare

  • Free or bulk-billed doctor appointments
  • Subsidised medications (PBS)
  • Accessible clinics for people with disability
  • Build hospitals/clinics in remote areas
  • Mobile health clinics
  • Expanded telehealth online services
  • After-hours services
  • Translators / bilingual staff

Improving Health Literacy

  • Clear, simple language — avoid jargon
  • Use visual images, not just words
  • Develop culturally relevant resources
  • Offer resources in multiple formats
  • Translate materials into multiple languages
  • Provide workshops to develop health skills
  • Community health education programs

Ottawa Charter Action Areas

Purpose:

Promotes a broad, supportive approach to improving health for everyone by focusing on supportive policies, environments, education, community involvement, and preventative care.

📚

Developing Personal Skills

Education and information to increase life skills and support personal development for health choices.

🏛️

Building Healthy Public Policy

Policies across all sectors that support healthy choices — taxes, legislation, regulations.

🌳

Creating Supportive Environments

Physical, social, economic, and political environments that are safe and stimulating — health-enabling places.

🤝

Strengthening Community Action

Empowering communities to set priorities, make decisions, and implement strategies for better health.

🏥

Reorienting Health Services

Shifting focus from treatment to prevention and health promotion — beyond clinical care.

Consumer Health

```

Health Literacy

Definition: The ability to obtain, read, understand, and use healthcare information to make appropriate health decisions.

Link to Health Status: Higher health literacy → better ability to navigate the health system, follow treatment plans, prevent disease, and achieve better health outcomes.

Health Indicators

  • Life Expectancy: Average number of years a person is expected to live from birth
  • Mortality: Death — death rates for specific causes or populations
  • Morbidity: Illness — rates of disease/injury in a population
  • Infant Mortality Rate: Deaths per 1,000 live births
  • Burden of Disease: Impact of illness in terms of disability and premature death (DALYs)

Australia vs Developing Countries — Health Indicators

IndicatorAustraliaDeveloping Countries
Life Expectancy~83 years (one of highest globally)Often 50–70 years; varies widely
Infant Mortality~3–4 per 1,000 live birthsCan be 30–100+ per 1,000
Leading Causes of DeathChronic diseases (CVD, cancer)Communicable diseases, maternal/child mortality
Access to HealthcareUniversal (Medicare), high coverageOften limited, inequitable, user-pays
Health LiteracyGenerally higher but gaps existOften lower due to education gaps
Sanitation/WaterHigh quality, widely availableSignificant gaps; linked to disease burden

Skills for Positive Health

```
Self-Management

Assertiveness

Expressing thoughts, feelings, needs, and boundaries clearly, confidently, and respectfully — standing up for oneself while respecting others' rights.

Self-Management

Resilience

Ability to adapt, recover, and grow stronger in the face of challenges. The ability to "bounce back" — maintaining wellbeing despite difficulties.

Self-Management

Stress Management

Strategies to cope with and reduce stress — identifying sources, applying methods to minimise negative effects on physical, mental, and emotional wellbeing.

Interpersonal

Communication & Collaboration

Ability to clearly share ideas, listen actively, work cooperatively, and build positive relationships in health settings.

Interpersonal

Cultural Competence

Understanding and respecting cultural and language influences on relationship building in health settings — tailoring communication to cultural context.

Culture & Health Decisions

Culture shapes health decision-making around organ and tissue donation, childbirth practices, blood transfusions, and end-of-life care. World events also influence personal, social, and cultural identity, affecting health behaviours.

Health Inquiry Process

```
Purpose:

A structured process for understanding and improving the health of populations — used to identify health needs, inform policy, and guide planning.

Planning a Health Inquiry

Identify and analyse a health issue. Develop focus questions to guide research. Good focus questions are clear, specific, researchable, and open-ended.

Using a Range of Information Sources

Identify reliable sources using criteria: currency, authority, accuracy, purpose, relevance (CAAPR). Use primary and secondary sources; quantitative and qualitative data.

Interpretation of Information

Summarise information, identify trends and patterns in data, develop evidence-based conclusions, and construct a supported argument.

Presentation of Findings

Choose a format appropriate for the audience — essay, report, oral presentation, poster. Consider language, visuals, and level of detail for the audience.

Criteria for Reliable Information Sources

CriterionWhat to Look For
CurrencyHow recent? Is the information up to date?
AuthorityWho wrote it? What are their credentials?
AccuracyIs it evidence-based? Referenced? Peer reviewed?
PurposeWhy was it written — inform, persuade, sell? Potential bias?
RelevanceDoes it directly address your health issue?

National Strategic Framework for Chronic Conditions (NSFCC)

```

What are Chronic Conditions?

  • Long-term and persistent
  • Gradual onset
  • Compromise quality of life
  • Examples: CVD, diabetes, osteoporosis, cancer, asthma, mental illness

Purpose of NSFCC

Provides guidance for the development and implementation of policies, strategies, actions, and services to address chronic conditions and improve health outcomes by preventing and managing chronic conditions.

Three Objectives

1️⃣ Focus on Prevention for a Healthier Australia ▼
  • Promote health and reduce risk factors
  • Create partnerships for health
  • Address chronic conditions at critical life stages
2️⃣ Provide Efficient, Effective and Appropriate Care ▼
  • Provide continual, coordinated care
  • Ensure access to appropriate health services
  • Share information freely between providers
  • Support people to optimise quality of life
3️⃣ Target Priority Populations ▼
  • Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander health
  • Aim for action and empowerment of disadvantaged groups
  • Address inequities in chronic disease burden

Principles of NSFCC

👤 Person-Centred

Values needs of individuals, carers, and families. Care is tailored to the person.

⚖️ Equity

Safe, high-quality healthcare for all people, regardless of background.

🚪 Access

Services are appropriate, available, and affordable for all who need them.

♻️ Sustainability

Long-term, responsible management of resources for future generations.

🤝 Collaboration

Cooperate and partner across sectors to achieve greater health impact.

📊 Accountability

Transparency and accountability — achieving best value in public interest.

🔬 Evidence-Based

Use epidemiological data and best practices to inform decisions.

🌐 Shared Responsibility

All parties — individuals, families, communities, governments — understand and fulfil their roles.

```
