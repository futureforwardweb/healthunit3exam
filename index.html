Holistic Health

Dimensions · Data types · Health inequity
Key Idea

Holistic health considers ALL dimensions of wellbeing — physical, mental, social, and spiritual — and how they interact. No single dimension exists in isolation.

🫁 Physical Health

The condition of the body — absence of disease, physical fitness, nutrition, adequate sleep, and healthy bodily functioning. The foundation all other dimensions build upon.

🧠 Mental & Emotional Health

Managing emotions, coping with stress, maintaining a positive outlook, forming a stable identity. Includes psychological resilience and emotional regulation.

🤝 Social Health

Quality of relationships and ability to interact positively with others. Includes a sense of belonging, communication skills, and community connection.

✨ Spiritual Health

Sense of purpose, values, and meaning — may or may not be religious. Includes cultural identity, connection to community, and a sense of place and belonging.

Health Inequity vs Inequality

⚖️ Health Inequity

Unfair, avoidable differences in health outcomes between groups — caused by social, economic, or environmental disadvantages. Inequities can and should be addressed.

📊 Health Inequality

Measurable differences in health status between groups — not necessarily unfair (e.g. older people have higher mortality). Inequality describes; inequity judges fairness.

Factors Creating Inequity

  • Income and socioeconomic status
  • Geographic location (rural/remote)
  • Cultural and language barriers
  • Discrimination and stigma
  • Low education and health literacy
  • Historical trauma (esp. First Nations)

Social Determinants of Health

  • Education and health literacy
  • Employment and income
  • Safe housing and environment
  • Access to healthcare services
  • Social inclusion and support networks
  • Early childhood development

Socio-Ecological Model of Health

Five Nested Levels
Each level influences health, from the individual outward to society
🧠 Individual

Personal characteristics: knowledge, attitudes, beliefs, genetics, skills, health literacy, personal behaviours, decision-making, personality, self-control

👨‍👩‍👧 Interpersonal

Social networks and relationships: family, friends, peer groups, peer pressure, support networks, role models — all influence health behaviours

🏫 Organisational

Structures and policies within organisations: workplaces, schools, sports clubs, healthcare settings — rules, culture, and programs shape behaviour

🏘️ Community

Community characteristics: neighbourhood safety, cultural norms, local facilities and resources, community organisations, physical environment

🌏 Society / Policy

Broad structures: laws and regulations, government funding, health campaigns, economic systems — public policy shapes the environments in which all individuals live

Purpose

Recognises that health is shaped by multiple interacting levels simultaneously. The most effective health interventions target several levels at once.

Specific Populations

Access & Equity issues for distinct population groups

🚪 Access

The ability of individuals and communities to obtain and use healthcare services when needed — about whether services are physically reachable and available.

⚖️ Equity

Just distribution of health resources, opportunities, and outcomes — ensuring everyone has the ability to achieve their highest possible level of health.

🏜️ Rural & Remote Australians ▼

Access Issues: Limited healthcare facilities, very long travel distances, healthcare provider shortages, lack of specialists, unreliable internet for telehealth.

Equity Issues: Higher rates of chronic disease and injury, lower insurance coverage, fewer specialised services, reduced life expectancy compared to metropolitan Australians.

👴 Elderly ▼

Access Issues: Mobility and transport challenges, aged care shortages, digital divide limiting telehealth, difficulty navigating complex health systems.

Equity Issues: Age-related discrimination (ageism), higher prevalence of chronic illness, financial constraints on fixed incomes, social isolation.

🪃 First Nations People ▼

Access Issues: Remote and very remote locations with limited healthcare infrastructure, chronically underfunded services, lack of culturally safe care.

Equity Issues: Intergenerational historical trauma, significantly higher rates of chronic disease, lower life expectancy (~8–10 years less), systemic racism and discrimination.

🏳️‍🌈 LGBTIQ+ ▼

Access Issues: Discrimination or fear of discrimination from providers, lack of culturally safe and affirming care, reluctance to disclose identity.

Equity Issues: Higher rates of mental health issues, depression, anxiety, suicidality; impacts of homophobia/transphobia; lack of inclusive health programs.

🔒 People in Prison ▼

Access Issues: Restricted access to specialists, limited healthcare choices, inadequate mental health and dental support.

Equity Issues: Higher rates of mental illness, substance use disorders, and communicable diseases; social marginalisation and poor health outcomes post-release.

🌍 Culturally & Linguistically Diverse (CALD) ▼

Access Issues: Language barriers, lack of culturally appropriate and translated resources, low English-language health literacy.

Equity Issues: Cultural differences in health beliefs, discrimination, socioeconomic disadvantage, reliance on community networks for health information.

👶 Children, Youth & Mothers/Babies ▼

Access Issues: Dependence on adults to access care, gaps in school-based health services, maternal health service gaps in regional and remote areas.

Equity Issues: Childhood poverty affecting development, adverse childhood experiences (ACEs), vulnerability to long-term health impacts from unmet early needs.

🎖️ Veterans ▼

Access Issues: Navigating complex DVA systems, geographic isolation post-service, difficult transition from structured military environment.

Equity Issues: High rates of PTSD, TBI, mental health conditions, and suicide risk; barriers created by military culture around help-seeking.

📊 Epidemiological Data

Used to identify health needs of specific populations — includes morbidity (illness rates), mortality (death rates), life expectancy, and other health characteristics. This data drives needs assessment and resource allocation decisions.

Types of Need

Felt · Expressed · Normative · Comparative
Purpose

Used in healthcare, social services, and community development to assess and address people's requirements — identifying gaps, prioritising resources, and planning services effectively.

Felt Need

Felt Need

Definition: A subjective need an individual perceives or feels they have — not yet acted upon.

Example: Person with chronic back pain feels they need physiotherapy but hasn't sought treatment.

How to determine: Community surveys, focus groups, community meetings, social media monitoring.

Expressed Need

Expressed Need

Definition: A felt need that has been acted upon — the individual has taken steps to seek help.

Example: Person with back pain visits physiotherapist, expressing their need for treatment.

How to determine: Service usage data, waiting lists, hospital admissions, demand for public programs.

Normative Need

Normative Need

Definition: A need defined by experts or standards — based on public health guidelines or best practices, not individual perception.

Example: Recommended daily fruit and vegetable intake from Australian Dietary Guidelines.

How to determine: Official guidelines, expert consultation, epidemiological data, safety regulations.

Comparative Need

Comparative Need

Definition: Determined by comparing groups to identify gaps in service provision between them.

Example: Rural town with no mental health services compared to an urban area that has extensive services.

How to determine: Compare healthcare access data, government reports, health statistics, best practices.

Needs Assessment

7-step systematic process to identify and address health issues
Purpose

A systematic process to identify and address health-related issues within a population. Assists decision-makers to allocate resources efficiently and design interventions that improve health outcomes and reduce inequities.

Identifying Health Issues

Recognising health concerns affecting a specific population using epidemiological data. e.g. high rates of type 2 diabetes in a community.

Analysis of the Problem

Examining root causes, contributing factors, and risk factors. Assessing barriers to healthcare access. e.g. lack of healthy food options and exercise facilities.

Prioritising Issues

Ranking health problems based on urgency, impact, and feasibility using community input and data. e.g. diabetes prioritised as highly prevalent and preventable.

Setting SMART Goals

Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, Time-bound goals aligned with public health guidelines and population needs. e.g. reduce diabetes incidence by 10% over 5 years.

Determining Strategies

Identifying evidence-based interventions — policy changes, community programs, education campaigns. e.g. subsidised healthy food programs.

Developing Action Plans

Creating timelines, delegating responsibilities, allocating resources. Engaging health professionals, community leaders, and government agencies.

Evaluating Outcomes

Measuring success through epidemiological data and feedback. Adjusting strategies based on effectiveness. e.g. tracking diabetes screening rates.

Actions & Strategies

Addressing health inequity · Ottawa Charter for Health Promotion

🚪 Improving Access to Healthcare

  • Free or bulk-billed doctor appointments
  • Subsidised medications (PBS scheme)
  • Accessible clinics for people with disability
  • Build hospitals/clinics in remote areas
  • Mobile health clinics
  • Expanded telehealth online services
  • After-hours services
  • Translators and bilingual staff

📚 Improving Health Literacy

  • Clear, simple language — avoid jargon
  • Use visual images and infographics
  • Develop culturally relevant resources
  • Offer resources in multiple formats
  • Translate materials into multiple languages
  • Provide community workshops
  • Community health workers and navigators

Ottawa Charter for Health Promotion

Promotes a broad, supportive approach to improving health for everyone — focusing on supportive policies, environments, education, community involvement, and preventative care. (WHO, 1986)

Consumer Health

Health literacy · Indicators · Australia vs developing countries

📖 Health Literacy

The ability to obtain, read, understand, and use healthcare information to make appropriate health decisions and navigate the health system.

Link to health status: Higher health literacy → better prevention, treatment adherence, health decisions, and overall outcomes.

📊 Key Health Indicators

  • Life Expectancy: Avg. years expected to live from birth
  • Mortality: Rate of death in a population
  • Morbidity: Rate of illness or disease
  • Infant Mortality Rate: Deaths per 1,000 live births
  • DALYs: Disability-Adjusted Life Years — combines disability and premature death

Skills for Positive Health

Self-management · Interpersonal · Cultural skills

🗣️ Assertiveness

Expressing thoughts, feelings, needs, and boundaries clearly, confidently, and respectfully — standing up for oneself while respecting others' rights and opinions.

💫 Resilience

Ability to adapt, recover, and grow stronger in the face of challenges, stress, or adversity. The ability to "bounce back" — maintaining wellbeing despite difficulties.

🧘 Stress Management

Strategies and techniques to cope with and reduce stress — identifying sources of stress and applying methods to minimise negative effects on overall wellbeing.

🤝 Communication & Collaboration

Sharing ideas clearly, listening actively, working cooperatively with others. Essential for positive relationships in health and community settings.

🌏 Cultural Competence

Understanding and respecting cultural and language influences on relationship building in health settings — tailoring communication to cultural context.

🌸 Culture & Health Decisions

Cultural background shapes health decision-making around organ and tissue donation, childbirth practices, blood transfusions, and end-of-life care. World events (pandemics, conflicts, social movements) also influence personal, social, and cultural identity — and therefore health behaviours.

Health Inquiry Process

4 steps · Focus questions · Reliable sources · Presenting findings
Purpose

A structured process for understanding and improving the health of populations — used to identify health needs, inform policy, and guide service planning.

Planning a Health Inquiry

Identify and analyse a health issue. Develop focus questions — clear, specific, researchable, and open-ended to guide a thorough investigation.

Using a Range of Information Sources

Identify and use reliable sources evaluated using CAAPR criteria. Use both primary and secondary, quantitative and qualitative sources.

Interpretation of Information

Summarise information, identify trends and patterns in data, develop evidence-based conclusions, and construct a well-supported, logical argument.

Presentation of Findings

Select a format appropriate for the audience — essay, report, oral presentation, poster. Consider language complexity, visual aids, and level of detail.

National Strategic Framework for Chronic Conditions

NSFCC — Objectives · Principles · Chronic conditions

🩺 Chronic Conditions

  • Long-term and persistent
  • Gradual onset (not sudden)
  • Compromise quality of life
  • Examples: CVD, type 2 diabetes, osteoporosis, asthma, cancer, mental illness, arthritis

🎯 Purpose of NSFCC

Provides guidance for the development and implementation of policies, strategies, actions, and services to address chronic conditions and improve health outcomes by preventing and managing chronic conditions in Australia.

Three Strategic Objectives

1️⃣ Focus on Prevention for a Healthier Australia ▼
  • Promote health and reduce risk factors across the population
  • Create partnerships for health between sectors
  • Address chronic conditions at critical life stages (early childhood, adolescence, pregnancy, aged care)
2️⃣ Provide Efficient, Effective and Appropriate Care ▼
  • Provide continual and coordinated care across the health system
  • Ensure equitable access to appropriate health services
  • Share information freely and effectively between care providers
  • Support people with chronic conditions to optimise quality of life
3️⃣ Target Priority Populations ▼
  • Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander peoples health and empowerment
  • Action and empowerment of disadvantaged and high-risk groups
  • Address inequities in chronic disease burden across populations
